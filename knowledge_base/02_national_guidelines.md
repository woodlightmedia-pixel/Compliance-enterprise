# DOCUMENT 02: NATIONAL GUIDELINES – STATUTORY WORKPLACE STANDARDS

## 1. Legal Definitions (Equality Act 2010)
Under national legislation, discrimination and harassment are legally defined terms tied directly to protected characteristics.

### The Nine Protected Characteristics:
1. Race (including color, nationality, ethnic, or national origins)
2. Sex
3. Disability (physical and mental long-term health conditions)
4. Age
5. Sexual Orientation
6. Gender Reassignment
7. Religion or Belief
8. Pregnancy and Maternity
9. Marriage and Civil Partnership

## 2. Harassment vs. Bullying Under the Law
* **Harassment:** Unlawful under Section 26 of the Equality Act 2010 if the unwanted behavior relates directly to a protected characteristic. 
* **Bullying:** While "bullying" does not have a singular statutory legal definition, employers owe a statutory **Duty of Care** under the Health and Safety at Work Act 1974. Severe, unaddressed bullying that breaches the implied term of mutual trust and confidence allows employees to claim **Constructive Dismissal** at an Employment Tribunal.

## 3. Employer Liability & Preventative Duty
* **Vicarious Liability:** Employers are legally liable for discriminatory acts or harassment committed by their employees during the course of employment unless they can prove they took "all reasonable steps" to prevent it.
* **Worker Protection Act:** Employers are bound by a strict proactive legal duty to take active, reasonable preventative measures to eliminate sexual and discriminatory harassment from the workplace, including protection against third-party actors (e.g., clients or members of the public).
